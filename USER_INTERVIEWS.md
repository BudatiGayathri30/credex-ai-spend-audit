# User interviews (MVP discovery)

Three short discovery calls with people who match the early ICP: small teams paying for multiple AI seats and feeling “subscription sprawl,” not enterprise procurement.

---

## Interview 1 — Solo founder, pre-seed SaaS

**Role:** Founder / full-stack builder  
**Company stage:** Solo, ~$8k MRR, 2 contractors  
**Context:** Paying ChatGPT Team, Cursor Pro, and a Copilot seat “because it was bundled with GitHub anyway.”

### Direct quotes

* “I’m not sure I’m **over**-paying—I’m sure I’m **confused** about what I actually need per role.”
* “If you tell me ‘save $800/mo,’ I won’t believe it. If you tell me ‘here’s the one seat you can probably drop,’ I might act this week.”

### Surprising insights

* He cared less about perfect pricing math and more about **a defensible story** he could paste into a Notion doc for his part-time designer.
* Monthly spend was uneven (some months near $0 on a tool), which made him nervous about “automated” audits feeling judgey.

### Product / design changes influenced

* Strengthened copy and UI emphasis on **confidence scoring** and conservative savings language.
* Engine rules now **suppress aggressive recommendations** when spend-per-seat looks like a data anomaly.

## Interview 2 — Indie hacker, profitable micro-SaaS

**Role:** Indie hacker (two products)  
**Company stage:** Bootstrapped, profitable, no formal “IT” function  
**Context:** Shared ChatGPT login culture + official Cursor seats; worried about policy but more worried about cost drift.

### Direct quotes

* “The share link is actually the feature. I’ll send it to my cofounder before I’ll read another dashboard.”
* “I don’t want onboarding. I want **one screen** that makes me feel sane about the bill.”

### Surprising insights

* She assumed the product would try to **upsell consulting**; she said she’d bounce if the first screen felt like a sales letter.
* Wanted the email capture to feel **optional and honest** about why we ask (follow-up, not “unlock results”).

### Product / design changes influenced

* Prioritized **public share pages** + Open Graph previews as a trust amplifier, not a gimmick.
* Lead capture kept **after** results (no gating) with transparent microcopy about email use.

## Interview 3 — Student-led campus startup studio

**Role:** Lead engineer in a university accelerator cohort  
**Company stage:** Hackathon-to-startup pipeline, teams of 4–8  
**Context:** Heavy use of free tiers; occasional team plan trials; finance mentor asking for “budget line items.”

### Direct quotes

* “We rotate tools every semester. Your audit has to work when half the team is on **free** or **edu** stuff.”
* “If the form takes more than a minute, we’ll screenshot it and never finish.”

### Surprising insights

* They treated “seats” loosely (shared accounts). They wanted outputs that still read well when inputs are **approximate**.
* Mobile completion rate mattered: they’ll start the form on a phone between classes.

### Product / design influenced

* Audit form kept **short**, numeric defaults friendly, and textarea optional enough to feel fast.
* Responsive spacing and tap targets tightened on the audit section; smooth **scroll offset** under the sticky header for in-page anchors.

---

## Cross-cutting themes

* **Believability beats bold savings claims.**
* **Shareability** is distribution for this MVP.
* **Anomaly handling** is a trust feature, not edge-case polish.
